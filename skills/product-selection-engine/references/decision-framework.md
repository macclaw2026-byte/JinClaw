# Product Selection Decision Framework

Use the shared monitoring standard from `../../self-cognition-orchestrator/references/monitoring-and-retrofit-framework.md` so the final decision is supported by primary evidence, backstops, and named blind spots.

## Sequence

1. define mission
2. collect public methodology
3. collect trend signals
4. collect platform-behavior signals
5. estimate real demand quality
6. estimate competition density
7. estimate commercial viability
8. score and decide

## Decision buckets

### Pursue
Strong signals across demand, margin, and manageable competition.

### Test
Some promising signals, but uncertainty remains.

### Reject
Weak demand, poor margin, severe saturation, or operationally poor fit.

## Decision controls

For each recommendation, identify:

- primary supporting monitor
- backstop review if confidence is medium or lower
- false-positive risk
- false-negative risk
- most important blind spot still open

## Important rule

A trendy product is not automatically a good product.
A visible product is not automatically a selling product.
A selling product is not automatically a profitable product.


## Hard filters before recommendation

Before a candidate can land in Pursue or Test:

- filter out food / beverage / ingestible products by default
- filter out shampoo, body wash, medicine, health-regulated, and similar body-contact / regulated categories by default
- filter out strong brand/IP-dependent or infringement-prone ideas
- filter out concepts with weak manufacturing or sourcing plausibility

## Reverse abstraction path

A candidate may begin as a market signal rather than a finished product idea.

Allowed process:

1. observe strong demand signal
2. abstract the real selling point or pain point
3. search for comparable non-infringing products
4. check whether current manufacturing can plausibly produce it
5. only then decide pursue / test / reject


## Amazon / Walmart scoring preference

For Amazon and Walmart branches, prefer products with:

- steady orders rather than hype-driven spikes
- manageable competition
- low qualification / gating burden
- weak dependence on strong brand trust
- simple operational profile

A product that appears capable of tens to low hundreds of stable daily orders with survivable competition is often better than a hotter but brutally crowded category.
